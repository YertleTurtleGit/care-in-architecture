1

The graphic is divided into five spectrums, each representing a phase of curating planning. Accordingly, the graphic can be read as a chronogram in which the individual phases build on each other like a planning process. The spectrums are not necessarily linked to each other, but can also function independently as a strategy:

  • Initiate – Learning from the site
  • Dialogue – Creating Knowledge
  • Coordinate to site conditions – Tentative calibration
  • Intervene through care – Transition on site
  • Expose – Publishing

In addition, the process can be divided into ‘Act’ and ‘React’. Act describes taking action and can also be understood as activist work. ‘React’ describes responding to the previous phase of insight.

2

The spectrums encompass important strategies and practices that are effective mechanisms in the context of curating planning practice. The individual orbits show the connection between related aspects. However, the arrangement is not specified and can be configured.

3

Similarly, the connecting lines serve to highlight the interdependence within the subject complex. The composition is not to be understood as settled, but serves to evoke associations when viewed and is intended to encourage further connections to be made.

4

The orange colored aspects show projects, theories, studios, or important individuals listed draw connections to planning practice and provide examples of how the practices of curating landscapes can be implemented in reality.

5

In summary, the diagram is not a taxonomy but rather offers a range of planning practices that are intended to encourage adaptation in practice. The graphic uses a style that is not immediately understandable, in order to reflect the complexity of the range of topics. Taking interdependencies into account, ‘curating landscapes’ proposes an iterative design approach that responds to change with persistent design processes.
